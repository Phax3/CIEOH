Over the past 30 years, the calcium isotope system has emerged as an important complementary tool to be used alongside more commonly used isotopes in carbonate rocks (i.e., C, O, and trace elements) for use in reconstructing past ocean chemistry and carbon cycle dynamics in Earth’s geologic history. During the precipitation of calcium carbonate by shell-forming organisms, the lighter isotope, 40Ca, is preferentially incorporated, leaving seawater relatively enriched in 44Ca (Skulan et al., 1997). Fractionation due to carbonate mineralogy is based on the coordination number (defined as the number of anions that surround a single cation in a crystal structure) associated with different carbonate minerals. Minerals like aragonite with a higher coordination number (9) relative to minerals such as calcite and dolomite which have lower coordination numbers (6), are depleted in 44Ca resulting in larger fractionation between the solid and fluid (Gussone et al., 2005). The rate of carbonate mineral precipitation also has a significant impact on the resulting amount of fractionation between the solid and fluid state. As the precipitation rate of carbonate minerals increases the amount of fractionation decreases, resulting in lower δ44/40Ca values (Gussone et al., 2020). Diagenesis has been a source of uncertainty for geochemists due to changes it causes on the primary isotope signals of carbonate rocks, replacing the isotope records with inaccurate secondary signals. Pairing this newer isotope system with more widely used carbonate-based isotope systems allows for more certainty when considering carbonate sediments are formed and turn into rocks over longer timescales. More specifically, calcium isotopes have been used to study the effects of carbonate diagenesis on original geochemical signals. (Higgins et al., 2018).

The calcium isotope system is an effective tool used in conjunction with carbon isotope data as it can be used to confirm ocean acidification events associated with large scale carbon isotope excursions. Throughout Earth’s geological history several large carbon isotope excursions have been identified in relation to mass extinction events such as the End Permian, Late Ordovician, and the Eocene-Oligocene. While carbon isotopes are effective at locating these events in time, they cannot always be used to identify the cause for extinction events and further impacts over time (Silva-Tamayo et al., 2018). The calcium isotope system has the potential to clarify these uncertainties by reconstructing the seawater chemistry of shallow oceans during sediment deposition, allowing for the constraint of causes for extinction events associated with large isotope excursions (Lau et al., 2017). The δ44/40Ca system has been used to test the plausibility of ocean acidification driven mass extinction during the PT mass extinction and found that an observed decrease in the δ44/40Ca value of carbonate sediments during the PT boundary could be explained by global ocean acidification (Payne et al., 2010). A decrease in the saturation state of calcium carbonate minerals (Ω) during an acidification event results in the accumulation of isotopically depleted calcium in seawater from dissolving carbonate minerals, resulting in a negative δ44/40Ca Ca excursion in the sediments as seen during the PT boundary (Payne et al., 2010). Large scale carbon release events not only have a direct impact on carbonate mineral precipitation through changes to ocean chemistry but also by changes to ocean temperatures. Changes to water temperature alongside the Mg:Ca ratio of seawater have a direct effect on the polymorph of calcium carbonate which will precipitate for given conditions (Balthasar and Cusack, 2015). This relationship allows for the reconstruction of seawater temperature and Mg:Ca ratio based on the dominant form of calcium carbonate for a sedimentary package.

Looking at longer-time scales, beyond mass extinction events, subsequent studies have been able to use the δ44/40Ca system to determine the fraction of aragonite precipitation versus calcite precipitation during variable ocean chemistry states known as “calcite or aragonite seas” (Balthasar and Cusack, 2015). The carbonate mineral polymorph which will precipitate at a given time is controlled by the ratio of magnesium to calcium as well as the temperature of the seawater at that time. Knowing when and where the ocean preferentially produces aragonite or calcite allows for the distinction between global and local effects on carbonate platforms as well as paleoenvironmental conditions, providing more certainty when combined with isotope systems such as carbon, oxygen, and trace elements (Higgins et al., 2018; Silva-Tamayo et al., 2018). Calcium isotope fractionation between calcite and aragonite seas is predictable and quantifiable based on the fractionation of 44/40 Ca between the calcite and aragonite phases. During calcite seas the δ44/40Ca value of carbonate rocks formed throughout this period are expected to be higher compared to aragonite seas where the δ44/40Ca value of carbonate rocks is expected to be lower. This is due to the coordination number of each carbonate mineral which determines the isotope of calcium which is most favorable to be incorporated into the crystal lattice of the mineral. 
Post depositional changes to carbonate rock chemistry introduce large sources of uncertainty due to the overprinting of the original chemistry of the rock making it difficult to distinguish between syn and post depositional effects. When a fluid carries a relatively high or low δ18O value when it interacts with the rock or sediments, due to Rayleigh Fractionation, the fluid will impart its isotope value on to the solid phase until they reach an equilibrium state which may be closer to the value of the fluid than the original isotope value of the rock during deposition. Studies have shown that calcium isotopes in conjunction with magnesium isotopes can be used to determine the effect of marine diagenesis on other proxies of importance, such as oxygen and carbon isotopes, allowing for the creation of more accurate isotope records and therefore paleoenvironmental and chemical reconstructions (Ahm et al., 2018). 

In conclusion, the calcium isotope system provides an invaluable tool for use in the reconstruction of paleoenvironmental and chemical states of seas in earth’s history while simultaneously enhancing the accuracy of other widely used isotope systems. The impact of diagenesis on isotope values in carbonates has been a longstanding point of contention, but with the paired use of calcium isotopes with other common isotope systems it may now be possible to distinguish between primary and secondary isotope signals of the sediments. The calcium isotope system may also be used for distinguishing between local and global effects using the ratio of precipitation between calcium carbonate states (aragonite, calcite, dolomite) to provide information on the local chemistry of carbonate platforms. For this system to improve, more work will need to be done developing a comprehensive calcium isotope record through time to better constrain global geological events, largescale isotope excursions, and the evolution of global carbonate production systems.
